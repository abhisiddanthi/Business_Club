An Introduction

In 2006, Netflix, which was till then an online DVD rental service, introduced a machine learning and data mining competition for movie rating prediction. They offered $1 million to whoever improved the accuracy of their existing system called Cinematch by 10%. This competition continued till 2009 when one of the teams finally broke this barrier. Though Netflix never used this improved algorithm, the competition still remains fresh in the minds of data scientists throughout the world after so many years. But what exactly are recommender systems and what's so special about them that Netflix not only offered such a huge prize but also risked violating privacy laws by publicly releasing the private data of their customers. From cavemen recommending their comrades to stay away from that tempting but ultimately deadly fruit to present-day Buzzfeed's quizzes that determine which type of cheese you are (yes, that�s a real thing), recommender systems have inconspicuously been around since the very beginning of human society. Framing the problem

The problem of generating recommendations can be framed as a matching task. In Netflix�s case, it has a set of users and a set of movie titles on its platform. Their recommender system seeks to best match each person to the movies they are possibly going to like. One intuitively sees how an understanding of the person�s likes and dislikes and the characteristics of different movies might aid this matching task. The two broad paradigms of recommender systems - Collaborative Filtering and Content Based Filtering make use of this very intuition.

 

Collaborative Filtering

This strategy is founded on the premise that users who have previously agreed are more likely to agree in the future. As a result, people with similar historical interests are deemed to be part of a cluster and are expected to be interested in comparable material in the near future. Collaborative filtering uses the system's interactions and data from other users to filter information. As a result, it doesn't require anything other than the consumers' previous preferences for a group of things.

 

The most standard method used in Collaborative Filtering is called the Nearest Neighbourhood Algorithm. The basic idea is to find the most similar users to your target user (nearest neighbours) and weigh their ratings of an item as the prediction of the rating of this item for the target user.

 

Content Based Filtering

This paradigm is the content analogue of collaborative filtering. The system seeks to recommend items similar to the ones a user has previously liked. This is framed as a problem where highly correlated items must be grouped in the same genre and recommended to users subscribing to that genre. This approach is best suited when there is sufficient information available on the items but not on the users.

 

The two paradigms of recommender systems are closely intertwined and modern hybrid recommender systems making use of both are not hard to spot. Similarity measures, dimensionality reduction that helps distil the important partitions in data and clustering literature have all found extensive use in the domain. For the second part of our build, we realise that maybe if we did not have the rating vector X for each movie but have the correct Θ vector for each user, the exact same loss function will find us the best rating vector X. Note however that gradient descent will run updates on X and not Θ this time. Our algorithm is beginning to take shape.

 

We can go ahead with a recursive method, where we initialise Θ to random values, then find the best X vector for this Θ. We again use this X vector to find the best representation of Θ. This loop goes on until the representations of both X and Θ become static. Interestingly this isn�t required because gradient descent can have multiple objectives and in our model, it can update both X and Θ for us!

Towards more complex models

[The following section delves into advanced methods that the industry has embraced for generating recommendations. Readers who wish to avoid the fair bit of technical details can safely skip this section.]

 

The 1992 paper by researchers at Xerox Palo Alto Centre, Goldberg, David, et al. "Using collaborative filtering to weave an information tapestry." introduced a mail classification system, Tapestry, which coined the idea of numerically defining customer similarity and used a model similar to our build. By the end of the first 21st century decade, the central concept of similarity had been rehashed, owing to huge work done by e-commerce firms like Amazon.com and interest from the Netflix Prize. SVD had emerged as the single most important algorithm in the contest so far: an incremental approach to finding the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of a mostly-blank matrix. Steffen Rendle published his work on Factorization Machines(FM) in 2010 that utilised properties of sparsity which are inherent to recommender systems.

Neural network analogues soon followed, with Neural Collaborative Filtering and DeepFM which greatly enhanced the working of Collaborative Filtering and Factorisation Machines respectively by integrating a neural approach. The big bois had also made major gains in the meanwhile. Google open-sourced its Wide&Deep model, hailed as one of the first major breakthroughs of deep learning in recommendation systems.

 

 

 

Meta, (then Facebook :) ), also open-sourced its own model DLRM in 2019. The Deep Learning Recommendation Model for Personalization and Recommendation Systems uses embeddings to process sparse features that represent categorical data and a multilayer perceptron (MLP) to process dense features, then interacts these features explicitly using statistical techniques proposed by Rendel in Factorisation Machines. Finally, it finds the event probability by post-processing the interactions with another MLP. Recommender Systems have definitely embraced complexity very well!

 

 

 

 

Real-Life Applications

 

Social Media

The content in our social media feeds is one of the most common uses of recommender systems. We all spend hours looking through our Instagram feeds and never grow bored. The recommender system provides items that may be of interest to us based on our previous interests. Collaborative Filtering is the most prevalent strategy now employed by most social media organisations.

 

 

A major drawback of Collaborative Filtering is that when a new item is received, the model is not able to recommend that item to anyone until it has been rated by a substantial number of users. Similarly, for items in the queue that didn't collect a lot of data, the model tends to assign them less weight and recommends more popular items, resulting in popularity bias.

 

E-Commerce

E-Commerce websites are another common user of recommender systems. Based on the user's current and previous purchases, they accurately forecast and advise the things that the user may require. For example, if you're buying a phone, the website may advise you to purchase accessories such as a screen protector or a phone case. Using this method, websites are able to increase their consumers' cart value. One of the significant differences between this strategy and that utilised by social media recommender systems is that items on e-commerce websites can be clustered based on their type, price, and other factors. This is a crucial consideration because two people who share comparable preferences in scents may not share similar likes in clothing. Therefore e-commerce websites use the method of Content-Based Filtering which takes into account the item attributes along with the person�s interests.

 

One of the most popular companies to have utilised recommender systems for its meteoric rise is Amazon. It incorporates a very efficient product recommender system that has proved to be a boon for both the company as well as the consumers.

Sentiment analysis of comments is done using NLP techniques and comments with positive sentiments are recommended to more customers using Amazon.

 

Recommender systems: Contemplating about the future

Recommender systems are primarily based on machine learning and data analytics.

They run algorithms on massive amounts of curated data to draw conclusions. Data analytics and its implementation in recommender systems can have a variety of advantages for all the stakeholders, be it the company using it or the customers benefiting from its services.

 

However, not just product or service recommendations constitute the domain of recommender systems. The pace at which technology has been growing in the past few years has led to a lot of interesting and undoubtedly useful applications of the recommender system. To mention a few,

  1. Code recommendation and prediction (GitHub Copilot)
  2. Predictive text (Grammarly)
  3. Sentiment analysis

 

Recommender systems are still being developed across the world with a hope to improve them further and put them to use in varied domains with wider areas of application. The Auto RecSys is being developed to replace the present-day recommender systems.

 

Auto-RecSys

At present recommender systems require to be trained and tuned according to the data provided and task requirements, however with the amount of data increasing constantly and becoming more and more diverse, it gets more and more complicated and expensive to develop new models or tune pre-existing models. Hence, Auto RecSys is the cutting edge recommender system architecture that trains itself based on the data provided, it doesn�t require to be trained manually. To be precise, Auto RecSys is a modular, searchable pipeline architecture tailored for recommendation models, which can automatically be used for any type of data provided. With the increasing demand for fast and efficient operations, automation has become the future of everything.
